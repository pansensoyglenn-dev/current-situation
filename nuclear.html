The Nuclear Threshold — how close are we to the unthinkable?

For 80 years, since Nagasaki, no nuclear weapon has been used in war. That taboo is weakening. As conventional deterrence erodes, arms control frameworks collapse, and new tactical systems blur the line between conventional and atomic war, the threshold to nuclear use is lower than at any point since the Cuban Missile Crisis.

What is the nuclear threshold?

The nuclear threshold is the figurative line between conventional conflict and the first use of a nuclear weapon. It is not a fixed point — it shifts with military doctrine, technology, leadership psychology, and crisis intensity. Crossing it would shatter the 80-year norm of non-use, with consequences for escalation that no model fully predicts and no leader fully controls.

The concept encompasses not only the decision to fire, but the conditions states publicly or secretly accept as justifications — defense of territory, avoidance of regime collapse, signaling resolve, or deterring an adversary's conventional advance. These doctrinal conditions have quietly changed in multiple capitals since 2022.

// the current landscape

Russia revised its nuclear doctrine in November 2024, explicitly lowering the conditions for atomic use: any conventional attack "creating a critical threat to sovereignty" may now trigger a nuclear response — a dramatic broadening from prior policy. Pakistan fields tactical nuclear weapons (TNW) specifically to offset Indian conventional superiority, meaning a limited border incursion could theoretically provoke atomic retaliation. North Korea has enshrined pre-emptive nuclear strike in law. Meanwhile, New START — the last remaining US-Russia arms limitation treaty — expired in February 2026 with no successor, removing the last structural constraint on superpower arsenals for the first time since 1972.

The tactical nuclear danger

Tactical nuclear weapons (lower yield, battlefield range) are often characterised as "more usable" than strategic warheads. Russia possesses an estimated 2,000 tactical warheads — the largest TNW stockpile in the world. The US maintains approximately 200 B61 gravity bombs forward-deployed across five NATO countries in Europe. The belief that a "limited" nuclear exchange could be controlled and contained is widely considered among strategic analysts to be one of the most dangerous illusions in contemporary security thinking.

Every serious wargame and simulation conducted by institutions including RAND, CSIS, and the Naval War College finds that once any nuclear weapon is detonated in a conflict context — even a demonstrative low-yield airburst over uninhabited territory — the probability of further nuclear use rapidly escalates above 50%. Command structures fracture under pressure, information degrades, and retaliation instincts are often faster than deliberate decision-making.

"A nuclear war cannot be won and must never be fought." — Ronald Reagan & Mikhail Gorbachev, Geneva Summit, 1985. Today, military planners in multiple capitals actively game limited first use as if it were a controllable, reversible option.

Threshold-lowering factors in 2026

Doctrinal shifts
Russia, Pakistan, and North Korea have all explicitly lowered published nuclear use thresholds since 2022. US and NATO maintain "calculated ambiguity" — which can prevent miscalculation, but can also backfire by leaving adversaries uncertain about red lines.
Command & control pressure
In crisis conditions, some tactical systems may be pre-delegated to field commanders to prevent "decapitation" of command authority. This significantly increases the risk of unauthorized or accidental launch by lowering the decision threshold to the unit level.
Collapse of arms control
New START expired February 2026. The Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty collapsed in 2019. The Open Skies Treaty ended in 2020. The entire framework of verified nuclear restraint built since 1963 has been dismantled within a decade.
Conventional parity loss
States losing a conventional conflict — a militarily routed Pakistan, a collapsing front-line state — may use nuclear threats or detonations to coerce outside intervention or to prevent total defeat. Nuclear weapons become the weapon of the losing side.
New technological domains
Cyberattacks on early-warning radar systems, anti-satellite weapons blinding nuclear command-and-control, and AI-accelerated decision timelines all create "use-or-lose" pressures — where leaders fear their weapons will be destroyed before they can respond, incentivizing pre-emption.
Generational leadership change
Leaders who built their worldviews during the Cold War — who understood the existential risk of nuclear exchange viscerally — are being replaced by a generation with no personal memory of 1962 or 1983. The institutional knowledge of how close humanity has come is fading.

Escalation Ladders & Active Flashpoints

Three active or latent conflict zones involve nuclear-armed parties, with direct confrontation increasingly plausible. A fourth — Taiwan — does not involve declared nuclear powers on both sides, but draws in the US and China in ways that nuclear planners cannot ignore.

// ukraine — the hidden nuclear risk

Russia has threatened nuclear retaliation if conventional strikes "threaten its territorial integrity" — including occupied regions of Ukraine. Western nations have drawn implicit red lines around Russian chemical or nuclear use. A Russian nuclear detonation — even a demonstrative low-yield airburst over the Black Sea — would trigger a massive conventional NATO response, but no publicly articulated plan exists for what happens next. The danger of inadvertent escalation through misread intent is acute.

// south asia — the most probable nuclear kindling

Pakistan's entire deterrence architecture rests on the credible threat of first use against advancing Indian conventional forces. Following a major terrorist attack traced to Pakistan-based groups, India might launch "surgical strikes" inside Pakistan. Pakistan's military doctrine explicitly plans to respond with tactical nuclear weapons on its own territory to "shock" India into de-escalation. This is not a theoretical scenario — it has been rehearsed. A nuclear exchange in South Asia, even limited, could put 1–2 billion people within range of direct effects or multi-year nuclear winter fallout.

// middle east — israel, iran & threshold dynamics

Israel maintains an undeclared nuclear arsenal estimated at ~90 warheads. Iran is now assessed as a nuclear threshold state — with weapons-grade uranium enrichment breakout time measured in weeks. A conventional exchange escalating toward regime-threatening strikes on either side could trigger Israeli nuclear use under the so-called "Samson Option" — a last-resort strike if the state faces existential military defeat. US forces permanently stationed throughout the region would be exposed to any nuclear exchange in ways that risk direct superpower involvement.

// taiwan — the superpower trigger

A Chinese military operation against Taiwan would directly confront US treaty obligations and military presence. China has never declared a no-first-use policy toward Taiwan specifically, and its rapidly expanding arsenal introduces new uncertainty about Beijing's nuclear signaling during a crisis. A direct conventional exchange between US and Chinese forces in the Taiwan Strait would be the most dangerous nuclear-adjacent scenario involving great powers since 1962 — without the communication channels or mutual understanding built during the Cold War.

Nuclear Doctrines — State by State

Nuclear doctrine — the conditions under which a state will use nuclear weapons — is the most important strategic variable in threshold analysis. Recent years have seen significant doctrinal shifts, mostly in the direction of lower thresholds and broader conditions for use.

Russia
⚠ Threshold lowered — 2024
Updated Nov 2024: nuclear use now permitted if Russia or Belarus faces a conventional attack "creating a critical threat to sovereignty." Also permits nuclear response to conventional strikes launched with nuclear-armed state support — a clear message to NATO.
Pakistan
⚠ First-use — explicit TNW deployment
Full-spectrum deterrence: tactical nuclear weapons deployed to offset Indian conventional superiority. Nasr (Hatf-IX) short-range nuclear missile designed specifically for battlefield use. First-use threshold is deliberately ambiguous to maximize deterrent effect.
North Korea
⚠ Pre-emptive strike — codified in law
2022 law enshrines pre-emptive nuclear strike right if leadership believes an attack is imminent. Kim Jong-un has publicly threatened nuclear use in multiple contingencies. No functioning back-channel with US or South Korea currently exists.
United States
Calculated ambiguity
Will "only consider" nuclear use in "extreme circumstances" defending vital US interests — but explicitly does not rule out first use. Extended deterrence covers NATO allies and South Korea/Japan. 2022 Nuclear Posture Review slightly narrowed stated use conditions.
China
No-first-use (declared) — contested
Official no-first-use policy since 1964. However, expanding arsenal, new silo fields, and sea-launched capabilities suggest a shift toward a more robust deterrent posture that some analysts interpret as eroding the NFU commitment in practice.
India
No-first-use (declared)
Official no-first-use policy with "massive retaliation" response to any nuclear attack. However, 2019 statements from the Defense Minister suggested the NFU policy "depends on circumstances" — introducing deliberate ambiguity vis-à-vis Pakistan.

The Collapse of Arms Control

The architecture of nuclear restraint — built painstakingly between 1963 and 2011 — has been almost entirely dismantled in the past decade. The world in 2026 has fewer verified constraints on nuclear arsenals than at any time since the Kennedy administration.

1963 — Partial Test Ban Treaty
Banned nuclear testing in atmosphere, underwater, and outer space. First multilateral nuclear restraint agreement. Still in force — but not signed by China, France, or India.
1968 — Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)
191 signatories. Foundation of the global non-proliferation regime. But three nuclear states (India, Pakistan, Israel) never joined, and North Korea withdrew in 2003. The five recognized nuclear states have made minimal progress on disarmament obligations under Article VI.
1987 — INF Treaty
US-Soviet agreement eliminating intermediate-range nuclear missiles — the most dangerous first-strike weapons. Collapsed 2019 after US accused Russia of violations. China was never a party and has deployed hundreds of INF-range missiles since.
1992 — Open Skies Treaty
Allowed unarmed surveillance flights over signatory nations — a critical confidence-building and verification tool. US withdrew 2020; Russia withdrew 2021. Eliminated a key layer of strategic transparency.
2011 — New START Treaty
Last remaining US-Russia strategic arms limitation treaty. Capped deployed strategic warheads at 1,550 each and allowed on-site inspections. Russia suspended participation in February 2023. Treaty expired February 2026. No successor framework under negotiation.
2021 — Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons
Entered into force with 93 signatories — but no nuclear-armed state has signed or ratified it. Represents strong normative pressure from the non-nuclear majority but has no enforcement mechanism against the states that matter most.

"The world is sleepwalking into a nuclear catastrophe. The warning signs are everywhere. Yet the political will to step back from the brink remains absent in the capitals that matter most." — UN Secretary-General, 2025 Disarmament Report

How Close Are We Today?

The Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists set the Doomsday Clock at 89 seconds to midnight in January 2024 — the closest it has ever been in the 77 years since its creation. The decision incorporated the Ukraine war, worsening South Asian tensions, the erosion of arms control, and the rapid modernisation of multiple nuclear arsenals simultaneously. When the clock was first set in 1947, it stood at seven minutes to midnight.

More leaders in more capitals explicitly believe nuclear weapons are "usable" in limited scenarios than at any time since the early 1980s. Strategic communication — the back-channels, hotlines, and shared understandings that reduced accidental risk during the Cold War — is weaker. The institutional memory of how close humanity came in 1962 and 1983 is fading from the rooms where decisions are made.

Crossing the nuclear threshold would not be "the end of the world" in the cinematic sense of total extinction. But it would end the world as it has existed since 1945: the post-war order, the 80-year taboo, the assumption that nuclear weapons are weapons that cannot be used. That crossing would open a door that no one knows how to close — to further use, to a global radiation health crisis, to nuclear winter effects threatening agriculture for billions, and to a new strategic order in which the nuclear firebreak no longer exists.

Understanding the threshold — who controls it, what moves it, and what might push us across it — is no longer a specialist concern. It is the central strategic fact of this decade, and the most consequential question of our time.
